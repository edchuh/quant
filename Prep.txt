SFM appeals to me because it combines a hedge-fund investment mindset with permanent, flexible capital and a highly institutional technology and risk platform. The firm can act decisively when conviction is high, but it is not constrained to one strategy, asset class, or short evaluation horizon.

The QDS role is particularly attractive because it sits close to portfolio managers and connects quantitative research, real-time P&L and risk, signal implementation, and production technology. That is a direct extension of what I currently enjoy most—working with rates and FX traders, diagnosing valuation and P&L issues in real time, and improving the systems that support risk decisions.

I am also drawn to the owner’s-mindset and constructive-debate culture. My strongest contributions have come when I questioned an output, investigated the underlying cause, communicated across teams, and improved the process rather than simply producing a one-time answer.








I am naturally drawn to cultures that value intellectual rigor, evidence, and independent thinking. My approach is highly analytical: I prefer to form decisions through research, data, and a clear understanding of expected value rather than relying primarily on intuition.

At the same time, I recognize that markets do not offer certainty. The objective is not to eliminate uncertainty, but to determine whether an opportunity has a genuine edge, understand the assumptions behind that edge, and manage the downside when those assumptions are wrong. I see quantitative analysis as a way to make investment judgment more disciplined, transparent, and repeatable.

My experience playing Go has shaped this mindset. I am more of a strategic planner than someone who focuses only on immediate calculations. I think about positioning, second-order consequences, how an opponent may respond, and how small advantages can compound over time. I approach investment and quantitative research similarly: understand the structure of the problem, identify the key drivers, test them rigorously, and adapt as new information arrives.

In my next role, I want to build something that I can genuinely take ownership of—whether that is a research methodology, a risk framework, a trading signal, or a portfolio-management tool. I am most motivated when I can understand a process deeply, improve it, and see it influence real decisions.

I also enjoy reverse-engineering successful approaches. That does not mean copying an outcome; it means identifying which components of a process are genuinely responsible for its success, testing whether they are robust, and finding ways to improve them. The culture I am looking for is one where people can challenge existing methods constructively, support their views with evidence, and remain humble enough to change their minds when the evidence changes.
